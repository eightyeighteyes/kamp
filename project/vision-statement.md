# Kamp

## What is Kamp?

Kamp is a cross-platform music player centered around having a joyful listening experience while supporting real humans making music.

Kamp is extensible, beautiful, easy to get started with and fun to settle into.  

## Guiding Lights

### Listening Experience

Listening to music is fun. Actively listening to music is even more fun. Actively listening to music surrounded by visuals the artist provided? Yes. More of that.

The out-of-the-box experience puts albums at the forefront. The first thing a user sees is a welcome screen that invites them to tell Kamp where their music library is, or create one for them.  When Kamp discovers their library, it creates the first run layout: a list of artists in a panel on the left and an album browser showing off the beautiful artwork of their collection in the middle.

### Supporting Artists

We think the shift to streaming has resulted in an unprecedented explosion in discoverability and accessibility. However, this has come at the cost of moving revenue away from creatives and towards technologists.  We are moving to a place where increasingly it is unsustainable to make a life out of artistic persuits.

We love music. Music surrounds us, holds us up, comforts us, and generally makes our life worth living. As such, we should be supporting artists.

This is why we have dedicated features that integrate with music marketplaces, starting with Bandcamp.  Bandcamp, Nina, and other upstart services focused on artist development are the kind of thing we want to see more in the world, and so we want to be an unofficial companion to these organizations.

We want to help people shift their habits from mindlessly streaming what some curated algorithm feeds them to being engaged, supportive listeners.  Once core music player and library management features are in place, we will start to build out features that are focused on integration with likeminded services -- discovery, library syncing, etc.

### Extensibility

All Kamp features are extensions, and Kamp lends itself to extensibility. We don't know what people will create for this, but we do know that as a community becomes involved, they'll want to add things we couldn't possibly think of: visualizers, discovery engines, whatever.

All of our features are extensions too, so users can turn them on or off as they feel. If we can't develop a feature with our own SDKs & APIs, then developer creativity is blocked.

If you're not a developer and just want to customize the look and feel of your player, we support that too.  We take design notes from IDEs such as VS Code and IntelliJ by arranging the application in customizable panels that a user can add and remove. Additionally, a user can define multiple views: out of the box, we have a Library view that centers around search and discovery, and a Now Playing view that shows off the art of what's currently playing.

You want your transport on the bottom? That's where I like it too. What if you want it on the top, iTunes/Apple Music style?  Great, the transport is a panel too: move it where you want it!
